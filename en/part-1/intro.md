# Part I: Getting Started — The Shape of FDE Engineering

> Scope: General / starting point for both tracks
>
> This Part is the "map" for every FDE. By the end, you should be able to answer three questions:
> 1. What does an FDE's real schedule actually look like?
> 2. Which three iron rules decide whether you do the job well?
> 3. Are you currently doing "data-driven FDE" or "LLM-driven FDE" work — and how do you switch?

---

## What This Part Solves

For a lot of people, the early confusion when they take on FDE work isn't "I don't know the tech" — it's "**I don't know where my time should be going**."

Does meeting with the customer count as work? Does writing a demo count as delivery? Does building an Eval set count as real work? Does pulling Ops into a Slack channel to wire up SSO fall inside the FDE remit?

Part I doesn't teach any specific technology. Its only job — before you open the IDE — is to set up a coordinate system in your head for "what an FDE engineer does in a week, how to tell right from wrong, and which class of situation you're currently in."

The remaining six Parts are built on this coordinate system.

## Chapters

- **Chapter 1: The FDE Workflow.** A weekly schedule plus a quarterly rhythm, contrasted across the two tracks (data vs LLM).
- **Chapter 2: The Three Iron Rules.** Sell outcomes / Fix Forward / Eval-driven — the three rules that draw the line between getting it right and getting it wrong.
- **Chapter 3: Two FDE Modes.** Data-driven (Palantir / AWS GenAIIC style) vs LLM-driven (OpenAI / Anthropic style) — and when to switch between them inside the same project.

## Relation to Other Parts

- No prerequisites: this is the start of the book.
- Forward links: the "three iron rules" from Ch 2 thread through Parts II–VII; the "two modes" framing in Ch 3 will determine whether you weight Part III (LLM) or Part IV (field delivery) more heavily later.
